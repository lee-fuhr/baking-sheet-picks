Baking Sheets questions, answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Half sheet pans (roughly 18 by 13 inches) are the most versatile size for home bakers. Look for sturdy aluminum or steel pans with rolled rims to resist warping. Avoid nonstick coatings if you want pans that last for years.

What size is a half sheet pan?

Half sheet pans measure approximately 18 inches long by 13 inches wide, with a rim about an inch high. That makes them the standard for most home ovens – they fit comfortably on a single rack while leaving room for air circulation. Quarter sheet pans (roughly 13 by 9 inches) are also common for smaller batches, but the half sheet is the workhorse. A full sheet pan (around 26 by 18 inches) is too large for typical residential ovens, so stick with half sheets for everyday baking.

What material should I choose for baking sheets?

Aluminum is the gold standard because it heats evenly and quickly. Look for heavy-gauge aluminum – it won’t warp or develop hot spots the way thin pans do. Some aluminum pans come with a silicone or ceramic coating for easy release, but plain aluminum develops a natural patina over time that actually improves nonstick performance. Carbon steel and stainless steel are also options. Carbon steel is durable and conducts heat well but can rust if not dried properly. Stainless steel is rust-proof but often has weaker heat distribution unless it has an aluminum core. For most home bakers, a heavy-gauge aluminum pan offers the best balance of performance and value.

Rimmed pans vs. rimless cookie sheets: which should I buy?

Rimmed baking sheets (often called jelly-roll pans) have a raised edge on all four sides. That rim is indispensable for roasting vegetables, catching drips from a lasagna, or baking sheet cakes. If you only buy one pan, make it a rimmed half sheet. Rimless cookie sheets (sometimes called baking trays) have no edge at all, or just a very low lip on one or two sides. They make it easier to slide cookies off onto a cooling rack, and they allow better air flow around the cookies. But they’re useless for anything with liquid or grease. If you bake cookies frequently, a rimless sheet is worth having – but start with a rimmed pan first.

How can I keep my baking sheets from warping?

Warping happens when a pan heats unevenly, causing the metal to expand and buckle. Look for pans with rolled rims – a thick wire edge that runs around the top – because that extra structure resists twisting. Thicker gauge metal (around 18-gauge or heavier) also stays flatter longer. Avoid putting a cold pan directly into a hot oven, and never blast the oven with the pan sitting on the rack during preheat. Let the pan come to room temperature first, and use the middle rack for the most even heat. Over time, even the best pans may develop a slight dome, but a rolled rim keeps it manageable.

Do I need nonstick baking sheets?

Nonstick coatings on baking sheets are convenient at first, but they tend to scratch and peel after a year or two of regular use. Once the coating is damaged, the pan becomes harder to clean and may release chemicals into your food. Many experienced bakers prefer plain aluminum, which becomes more nonstick over time as oil and heat build up a natural seasoning. If you do choose nonstick, hand wash only and avoid metal utensils. Silicone baking mats or parchment paper can give you nonstick performance on any pan without the coating risks. That approach also lets you buy uncoated pans, which last practically forever.

What’s the best way to clean a baking sheet?

For everyday cleaning, hot soapy water and a non-abrasive sponge are enough. Stubborn baked-on residue can be loosened by sprinkling baking soda over the pan, adding a little water to make a paste, and letting it sit for 15 minutes before scrubbing. For really stubborn spots, a paste of baking soda and hydrogen peroxide works well. Avoid putting your pans in the dishwasher if possible. The harsh detergents and high heat can accelerate discoloration (especially on aluminum) and damage nonstick coatings. Hand washing preserves the surface and extends the pan’s life. If you use uncoated aluminum, a little Bar Keepers Friend can restore shine without ruining the metal.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use a half sheet pan for both baking and roasting?

Absolutely. A half sheet pan’s rim is high enough to contain juices from roasted chicken or vegetables, and its size fits most ovens. It’s the most versatile pan in a home kitchen, equally at home with cookies, sheet-pan dinners, and even small roasts.

Is aluminum safer than nonstick?

Plain aluminum is widely considered safe for cooking and baking. The link between aluminum and health concerns has been largely debunked by major health organizations. Nonstick coatings like PTFE are also safe when used properly (below 500°F), but they can deteriorate. Many bakers prefer aluminum because it’s durable and doesn’t introduce any coating concerns.

Why do my cookie sheets warp in the oven?

Warping is usually caused by thin metal and uneven heating. When a thin pan is placed on a hot rack, some areas expand faster than others, causing the metal to buckle. Choosing a thicker gauge pan with a rolled rim greatly reduces warping. Also, let the pan preheat with the oven instead of putting a cold pan into a hot oven.

Can I put baking sheets in the dishwasher?

We recommend hand washing your baking sheets. Dishwasher detergents can cause aluminum to dull and darken, and they can strip the seasoning from uncoated pans. For nonstick sheets, the high heat and harsh chemicals can break down the coating over time. A quick hand wash is gentler and keeps pans performing well.

What’s the difference between a cookie sheet and a baking sheet?

In casual use, the terms are often swapped. Technically, a cookie sheet is rimless (or has a very low lip) to allow easy sliding of cookies onto a rack. A baking sheet has raised rims on all sides and can hold liquids. Most home bakers need both, but a rimmed baking sheet is more versatile for general cooking.

Are insulated baking sheets worth buying?

Insulated sheets have an air gap between two layers of metal, which helps prevent cookie bottoms from overbrowning. They’re good for very thin cookies or if your oven runs hot. However, they can be heavy and expensive, and they don’t get as hot as single-layer pans, which some recipes depend on. For everyday use, a quality single-layer pan is a better investment.

In shortIf you take away one thing, it’s this: buy two heavy-gauge aluminum half sheet pans with rolled rims. They’ll handle almost everything you bake or roast, they’ll last for decades with basic care, and they’ll outperform pricier nonstick pans over the long haul. A little parchment paper or a silicone mat gives you nonstick convenience without the coating headaches. Stick with the basics, and your baking sheets will be the most-used pans in your kitchen.
