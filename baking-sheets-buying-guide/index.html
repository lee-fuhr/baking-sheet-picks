How to choose a baking sheets

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Look for heavy-gauge aluminum or steel half sheet pans with rolled rims. A set of two is ideal for most home bakers. Avoid thin, flimsy sheets that warp at high heat, and skip nonstick coatings if you want pans that last decades.

What size baking sheet should I buy?

The half sheet pan is the Goldilocks of baking sheets. It fits most ovens, holds a dozen cookies without crowding, and works for sheet-pan dinners. Quarter sheets are handy for smaller batches and toaster ovens, while full sheets are often too large for standard home ovens. A set of two half sheets covers nearly every need, one for baking, one cooling on the rack or prepping the next batch. If you bake often, adding a couple of quarter sheets gives you flexibility for frozen treats, one-pan sides, or serving.

What material is best for baking sheets?

Aluminum is the gold standard. It heats quickly, cools evenly, and doesn’t rust. Look for bare aluminum or aluminized steel, both conduct heat better than stainless steel. Carbon steel is durable but heavier and can rust if not dried immediately. Nonstick coatings are convenient but scratch easily and won’t last as long. A plain aluminum pan with parchment paper or a silicone mat gives you the same release without the eventual flaking. If you want a darker finish for extra browning, go with a heavy-gauge anodized aluminum pan.

Should I buy a set or individual pans?

A matched set of two half sheets is usually the smartest buy. You get consistent performance because both pans are the same material and thickness. Sets often cost less per pan than buying singles. If you already own one decent half sheet, a single complement in a different depth or gauge can be useful. But for most bakers, a two-pan set is the simplest route to even baking and fewer batch waits.

What features prevent warping and rust?

Warping happens when thin pans heat unevenly. The fix is a thicker gauge, look for pans made from heavy-gauge aluminum or steel, ideally with a reinforced rolled rim. A rim that is fully welded or rolled adds stiffness and doubles as a grip. Rust is a non-issue for aluminum pans. For steel pans, a silicone or enamel coating helps, but the best defense is drying them by hand after washing. Avoid the dishwasher for any baking sheet, the heat and harsh detergents accelerate corrosion and warping.

How do I care for baking sheets so they last?

Hand wash with warm water, mild soap, and a non-abrasive sponge. Never put aluminum sheets in the dishwasher. Dry immediately to prevent water spots (and rust if steel). For stubborn grease, use a paste of baking soda and water or a brief soak. Seasoning bare aluminum or carbon steel pans builds a natural nonstick patina over time. If you buy plain pans, expect them to darken with use, that’s normal and actually improves browning. Store sheets flat or nested with a layer of felt or paper towel to avoid scratches.

Are nonstick baking sheets worth it?

For most home bakers, no. Nonstick coatings wear out after a few years, and you can’t use metal utensils on them. A plain aluminum sheet with parchment or a silicone mat performs just as well and lasts indefinitely. The exception is if you bake delicate items like meringues or macarons that need to slide off without any lining. Even then, a well-seasoned bare pan can do the job. If you do buy nonstick, treat it gently, hand wash only, no abrasive pads, and stack with protective layers.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use a baking sheet for roasting vegetables and meat?

Absolutely. A rimmed half sheet pan is perfect for roasting, the rim catches juices and makes tossing vegetables easy. Just avoid very high broil temperatures if your pan is thin. For heavy jobs like a whole chicken, a thicker pan is worth the extra cost.

Do I need to line baking sheets with parchment paper?

Not always, but parchment makes cleanup faster and prevents sticking. For cookies, a silicone baking mat works even better because it’s reusable and provides even heat distribution. Skip foil, it can react with acidic foods and doesn’t release as cleanly.

Why do some baking sheets have rolled edges?

Rolled edges reinforce the pan, reducing warping, and provide a sturdy grip. They also prevent the rim from cutting into your hands or damaging countertops. Fully closed rolled edges are better than open or wire-reinforced rims because they don’t trap food or moisture.

What is the difference between a cookie sheet and a half sheet pan?

A cookie sheet usually has one or no raised edges, making it easy to slide cookies off. A half sheet pan has a shallow rim on all four sides, which contains drips and makes it versatile for roasting and baking. For most kitchens, a rimmed half sheet pan is more practical.

How many baking sheets do I need?

Two half sheets are the sweet spot for most bakers. You can rotate pans between racks for even baking, or prep one tray while the other is in the oven. If you bake large batches often, add two quarter sheets for smaller jobs or toaster oven use.

Are dark or light baking sheets better?

Dark pans absorb more heat, giving you darker bottoms and faster browning. Light pans reflect heat and produce more even, golden results. If a recipe calls for one, stick with it, otherwise, light or medium-grey pans are the most forgiving for a wide range of baked goods.

In shortThe best baking sheets are heavy, rimmed, and made of plain aluminum. Skip thin pans and nonstick coatings. Spend your money on a set of two half-sheet pans; with proper hand-washing and drying, they will last a lifetime and bake everything from cookies to sheet-pan chicken without fuss.
